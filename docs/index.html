CS 6241 Compiler Design

Compilers have always played a key role in the backbone of computing systems.

This course takes you through the foundational concepts of optimizing compilers and helps you get your hands on a production compiler toolchain, namely, our dear LLVM.

Step through the Compiler Museum

A short tour of the evolution of programming abstractions.

  • 1953-1958

    The visionaries of translation

    Grace Hopper and FLOW-MATIC

    Grace Hopper initially developed the A-0 system, which functioned more like a linker or loader than a true compiler. However, she continued with her work to develop B-0, later known as FLOW-MATIC that was one of the first English-like data processing languages. FLOW-MATIC was designed for business applications and readability.

  • 1957

    The optimizing compiler takes flight

    John Backus and FORTRAN

    With hardware getting expensive and human programmers even more so, John Backus led a team at IBM to develop FORTRAN (FORmula TRANslating System). This allowed scientists to write code that looked like algebraic formulas. FORTRAN proved that abstraction did not have to sacrific performance. This effectively launched the field of compiler optimization.

  • The Era of Formalization

  • 1960

    A language to unite all

    ALGOL and BNF

    An international committee of scientists developed ALGOL (ALGOrithmic Language) that became the standard for algorithm description used by the ACM. It introduced code blocks, recursion, and nested function definitions. John Backus introduced the Backus normal form, expanded by Peter Naur for ALGOL 60, and renamed Backus-Naur Form (BNF), a formal way to describe programming language syntax that is still in use today. BNF decouples the definition of a language from its implementation.

  • 1958-1959

    The Great Divide - Business vs. Science

    COBOL and Lisp

    COBOL was inspired by FLOW-MATIC and designed for data processing, prioritizing file handling and record management. Its English-like verbose syntax made it self documenting for business managers. In contrast, John McCarthy at MIT created LISP that introduced a radically different paradigm of functional programming. Its homoiconic structure, where code and data share the same representation, enabled powerful metaprogramming capabilities. LISP's design emphasized recursion and higher-order functions, making it a favorite in AI research.
